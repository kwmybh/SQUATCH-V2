Main Idea

At night, in the valley in Guernsey County, Ohio, Bea Mills took a thermal imaging scope and pointed it toward the pond. Through the scope, she saw two heat-map outlines that were obviously deer bending down to drink from the water. She asked the other searcher, “What’s that third thing?” The third thing, a blob, had stood up and started walking away.

The figure disappeared, and the larger group was alerted. They started watching the forest and Mills was the only one looking in her direction. She saw tree trunks and nothing else. Then one of the trunks began moving. It became a silhouette that looked like a very fit football player with broad shoulders and thin hips. It walked toward her, casually.

Main Idea - Extended

In fear, she hid her face in her hands. Then she heard what sounded like an elephant crashing through briars, brambles and water. The others also heard it too, but no one saw the creature.

Then, it was over.

The next day, they searched for footprints or other evidence but found nothing. Since then, Mills has started to study more about the forest and she’d require substantial evidence before considering someone’s description of a Sasquatch encounter. She also knows that her own experience, without evidence, wouldn’t pass her test.

“I know what I saw was completely surreal and burned into my brain

. . . but it’s just a story” - Mills

History

On October 20, 1967, Roger Patterson, a rodeo cowboy, and Bob Gimlin were amateur filmmakers working on a docudrama in Northern California. During their trip, Patterson saw something walking in the distance and captured about a minute of shaky footage from a rented 16mm movie camera showing a hairy human-like figure estimated to be more than 7 feet tall. The animal was very muscular and appeared to have human-like breasts. In the 50 years since the Patterson-Gimlin film and plaster casts of enormous footprints from the creature, the men made later have become the keystone Sasquatch evidence. One scientist speculates that the Patterson-Gimlin film is perhaps the most analyzed piece of footage behind the Zapruder film of President John Kennedy’s assassination.

In the mid-20th century, the field of cryptozoology – studying animals thought to be extinct, or others thought to be myths – grew in popularity. The book “The Skeptic Encyclopedia of Pseudoscience” allows that “Cryptozoology ranges from pseudoscientific to useful and interesting, depending on how it is practiced.” In 1994, an audio recording in Columbiana County, Ohio, near the Ohio River captured a mournful, repeating, moaning yell that became known as the Ohio Howl. In 2013, Joshua Stevens, a researcher at Penn State University, compiled a data map from more than 3,000 BFRO reported sightings from 1921 to 2012. The Ohio River Valley is particularly dense with sightings as is the Pacific Northwest and Central Florida. Stevens also noted a spike in reports between 2000 and 2009.
